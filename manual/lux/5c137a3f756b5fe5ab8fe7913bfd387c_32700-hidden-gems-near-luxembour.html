Hidden gems near Luxembourg

Though distant and sunny shores may beckon, you'd be surprised at the number of hidden gems closer to home, living in Luxembourg

sarita rao
30.12.2018— Last update: 30.12.2018, 10:00

Distant and sunny shores beckon. But you'd be surprised at the hidden gems closer to your Luxembourg home that are perfect for keeping the kids entertained during half-term – rain, shine or snow.

Maastricht

Explore the cobbled streets of Maastricht and the lovely Vrijthof Square. Browse books in the nearby Selexyz Dominicanen Bookstore, a thirteenth–century Dominican church converted into a shop, which The Guardian called the "best bookstore in the world".

Take a quiet moment in the Basilica of Saint Servatius or light a candle in the Basilica of Our Lady, walk around the fortifications to spot Hell's Gate built more than 800 years ago. There's also a Natural History Museum that contains the remains of dinosaurs and giant turtles, an aquarium and a working beehive.

Just outside the city, you can visit the town of Valkenberg with its famous castle, velvet caves, cable car and year-round toboggan ride.

There are several main-brand hotels like Novotel and Crown Plaza, but if you're looking for a kid-friendly place, the Townhouse design hotel gets good reviews.

Narvik Home Parc's Mooi Zutendaal has log cabins perfect for families, in a complex complete with nature trails, an indoor pool and play areas. Landal's Domein de Cauberg offers self-catering cottages and flats. Both destinations are less than a 30 minutes' drive to Maastricht.

Strasbourg

Setting of the European Parliament for six months, Strasbourg is a great place for a short break. The city's Cathedrale N0tre Dame soars over the old town and kids will love its famous astronomical clock.

The clock has animated figures which come out each day at 12.30 pm to present the stages of life, from child to elderly who all "parade past death," according to the city's tourism website.

Nearby Place Kléber, with its water fountains, is a good traffic-free space for kids to run about while you stop for lunch.

If you like a bit of history, take a stroll around Petite France's narrow canals flanked by half-timbered houses. Try an Alsatian delicacy or sit in a cosy beer snug.

Children can run off some steam in the Parc de l'Orangerie , which boasts several play areas, a lake where you can rent boats and a free mini-zoo with flamingos, monkeys, goats and the ubiquitous stork, the symbol of Strasbourg.

Strasbourg has a tram system and a 24-hour 'trio' ticket that provides unlimited travel for three people. It costs less than €10. Children under five travel for free.

If you prefer the comforts of a hotel, then the Mercure provides family rooms and an indoor swimming pool and is close to a tram stop.

Dijon

A bit further afield but full of charm, Dijon makes for a family break. Perhaps seen as more of a summer destination, there is, however, still lots to enjoy in the winter months, with the bonus of a string of beautiful historic villages and towns nearby.

The Musee de la vie Bourguignonne (free entry) has life-size displays of how the streets, shops and a family kitchen might have looked like in Burgundy a few hundred years ago.

There are plenty of hands-on activities to keep the children entertained, such as dressing up in nineteenth-century clothes or colouring in pictures of one of the display scenes.

For lunch, you can taste the region's signature dish, Boeuf Bourguignon, while the kids run around the fountain-filled and traffic-free Place de la Liberation. Need to walk off that lunch? Then the Jardin de l'Arquebuse, Dijon's Botanical Gardens, is a lovely place for a relaxing stroll. Situated at the edge of the Gardens is the Natural History Museum (free entry).

Weather permitting, you can rent bikes or zip wire through the trees (ages six and up) at Parc de la Colombieres, which also has farm animals and a jungle gym.

Fancy something very different? In nearby Alesia, you can visit the new Gallo-Roman Museum , which provides children's audio guides in several languages, reenactments from both the Gaul and Roman perspectives and the chance to try out sword fighting.

If your children are too small to appreciate the history of Vercingetorix, you can check them into the free crèche filled with toys and craft activities.

Dijon is a popular place to rent a holiday home, and you can find everything from small apartments to country houses for rent here. 

In town, the Appartement Jeannin & Antiquaires comes highly recommended.

The Black Forest

It's long been a popular destination and it's easy to see why. Magical nature parks, abandoned mines, fairy-tale castles and, of course, black forest gateaux are all great attractions.

To see indigenous wildlife, visit the Steinwasen Adventure Park near Freiburg, also home to a 218-metre long rope bridge, rides and other attractions. Need more animals? Try the Mundenhof Zoo (also in Freiburg). There's a bear sanctuary and wolf park in Schapbach.

Since the Black Forest is such a big place, stretching from Baden-Baden (so good they named it twice), to Freiburg, there isn't time to list everything, but some highlights include: numerous thermal springs, a trip to a Grimm fairytale castle Berg Hohenzollern and any number of silver mines (for example, the mine in the Mount Schauinsland region means taking Germany's longest cable car to reach the entrance).

And the region isn't short of hotels or holiday homes, so you're sure to find somewhere, even at the last minute.

If you are planning to go to the Black Forest in the spring, Europa Park (open from late March) is a rival to Disneyland but with slightly cheaper prices and a few less princesses. It's themed on countries in Europe (although not all of them) and has rides, shows and other attractions to keep children big and small happy.

Disneyland Paris

It's just over three hours' drive to Marne-la-Vallée, where you'll find Disneyland Paris's Magic Kingdom and a vast number of hotels to suit all budgets, both within and without the Disney complex.

The main reason to stay in-complex is the "magic hours" (their words not mine) you get before the park is open to everyone else.

The new Villages Nature (a Disney meets Centreparcs village) has a vast indoor/outdoor swimming pool with several water slides, a farm and treetop adventure park. It is served by a regular bus to Disneyland and the train station, as well as the Charles de Gaul airport.

Have fun, meet a princess, be a Pirate of the Caribbean, fly a rebel spacecraft in StarTours or free-fall in the Tower of Terror.

You can fast track certain rides to get timed tickets, which means you don't have to queue. Entry tickets are not cheap but the website has current offers here, You should shop around on group discount sites.

You are also close enough to Paris to take in some of the city's sights. The station at Marne-la-Vallée runs services to the heart of Paris (approximately 40 minutes one way) for a quick stop at the Louvre or the Eiffel Tower.

Bitburg to Bonn

This picturesque part of Germany is home to pretty towns like Bad Munstereifel, a beautiful medieval walled town, volcano parks, wildlife and fairytale castles.

The twelth century Eltz Castle sits on a secluded hilltop and is still owned by the same family, 33 generations on. Tours in English leave every hour. North of Bitburg, the Daun Wildlife Park invites you to drive the eight–kilometre dirt road through the woods to visit donkeys, buffalo, llamas, ostriches and wild boar. The monkey sanctuary lets you get up close and personal to our cousins, and there is an enormous playground equipped with trampolines and a reasonably priced restaurant.

Science enthusiasts will enjoy the Volcano Park in the Eifel region which exhibits volcanoes, archaeology and industrial history. It also hosts educational trails. The museum incorporates 25 stations that can be visited by road, cycle or foot. These include the Roman mine, the Andernach geyser and the Mendig lava dome.

Interactive exhibits bring out the little scientist in your children.

Much of Bonn was destroyed during World War II, but there are still some historical remains and the fantastic German National Museum of Contemporary History. German chancellor from 1969 to 1974, Willy Brandt's car, pieces of the Berlin Wall and a tiny fragment of rock from the moon are among the 7,000 objects on display.

The Saar region

So close it's easy to make a day trip.

.Bbut why not relax and take longer?

The town of Saarburg provides a lovely setting for a relaxed lunch.

Walk past the waterfall or take a cable-car trip to the nearby hilltop and an all-weather toboggan ride. The castle was built by Count Siegfried of Luxembourg in 946 and you can still walk around its ruins on a hill overlooking the town.

In nearby Merzig, the free Wolfpark Werner Freund is home to several species of wolves from Europe and the Americas. It offers numerous hiking paths through the forests.

In the Roscheider Hof Folklore and Open Air Museum in Konz, you can explore everyday life from the regions of Eifel, Hunsrück, Saargau and parts of Lorraine and Luxembourg.

There's a 1912 classroom and exhibitions on everything from dentistry to wine growing. Mettlach is home to the Villeroy and Boch museum.

Völklinger Hütte, old blast furnaces that are now a UNESCO heritage site in Völklingen, houses an art gallery and science centre focusing on the classical Greek four elements of earth, water, fire and air.

If sightseeing is starting to drag, visit Saarbrucken for some cut-price retail therapy at stores like Primark and TK Maxx, then wave a quick hello to the lemurs and meerkats at the Saarbrucken Zoo or take a pony ride at Wildpark Saarbrücken.
